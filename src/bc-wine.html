The Guide

BC Wine by Region

From the Gulf Islands to the South Okanagan desert. Select a region to explore the wineries we carry.

BC Wine Country

British Columbia has six distinct wine regions, each shaped by different geography, climate, and soil. The South Okanagan is where the big reds come from — desert conditions, long growing seasons, concentrated fruit. Move north toward Kelowna and the lake moderates everything, giving winemakers more range. The Similkameen sits west of the Okanagan and punches well above its size. The Gulf Islands and Vancouver Island are cooler, maritime, and better suited to whites, aromatics, and Pinot Noir. The wineries below are ones we carry at the shop. Select a region to zoom in.

Oliver / Osoyoos

The south end of the Okanagan Valley, where the landscape turns to semi-desert. Canada's only desert sits just south of Osoyoos, and the growing conditions here are unlike anywhere else in the country. Long, hot days. Cool nights. Soils ranging from sandy loam on the Black Sage Bench to clay and gravel on the Golden Mile. This is where BC's most serious reds come from — Syrah, Cabernet Franc, Merlot-based blends that can age. If you want to understand what BC red wine is capable of, start here.

Naramata Bench & Skaha

A strip of benchland running north from Penticton along the east shore of Okanagan Lake. The lake moderates the climate, stretching the growing season and keeping temperatures from pushing too hard in either direction. The result is wines with more tension and freshness than the south — elegant reds, whites with real structure, and some of the better rosé in the province. The Skaha Bench, just south of Penticton, sits in between Oliver and Naramata and benefits from both influences.

Kelowna

The commercial heart of BC wine country, but don't let that fool you. Some of the oldest vineyards in the province are here, and the best producers have been quietly building something serious for decades. Okanagan Lake has a moderating effect in both directions — warmer winters, cooler summers — which gives Kelowna the range to produce everything from sparkling wine to late-harvest dessert bottlings. Organic farming is increasingly common. If you know where to look, Kelowna rewards it.

Similkameen Valley

BC's smallest wine region and, for those paying attention, one of its most interesting. The valley runs east-west through the Cascade Mountains west of the Okanagan, with extreme diurnal temperature swings and gravelly alluvial soils that stress the vines in the best possible way. Organic farming is widespread here — something about the dry, windy climate makes it natural. A handful of producers have done more than anyone to establish what this region can do. Clos du Soleil is the clearest example.

Gulf Islands

The Gulf Islands are cool, maritime, and produce small quantities of wine from a cluster of boutique estate producers. The growing conditions are the opposite of the Okanagan — less heat, more rainfall, shorter seasons. The wines lean toward aromatic whites, Pinot Gris, and skin-contact styles. A Sunday in August, out of Salt Spring Island, is exactly the kind of producer this region attracts: small farming operation, natural winemaking, genuinely distinctive wine.

Vancouver Island

A growing scene, still finding its footing. The Cowichan Valley, just north of Victoria, is the most established sub-region. The climate is maritime and the seasons are shorter than the Okanagan, which means white wines and Pinot Noir are the safest bets. Averill Creek in the Cowichan Valley is among the most serious producers on the island — their Chardonnay and Pinot Noir are worth seeking out.
