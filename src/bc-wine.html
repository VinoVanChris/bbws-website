BC Wine by Region

From the Gulf Islands to the South Okanagan desert. Select a region to explore the wineries we carry. BC craft beer and distilleries below.

BC Wine Country

British Columbia produces wine across six distinct regions, each shaped by different geography, climate, and soil. The South Okanagan is where the big reds come from — desert conditions, long growing seasons, concentrated fruit. Move north toward Kelowna and Okanagan Lake moderates everything, giving winemakers more range. The Similkameen runs west of the Okanagan through the Cascades, small in size but serious in quality. The Gulf Islands and Vancouver Island are cooler, maritime, and better suited to whites, aromatics, and Pinot Noir. Select a region to zoom in on the wineries we carry.

Oliver / Osoyoos

The south end of the Okanagan, where the landscape turns to semi-desert. Canada's only true desert sits just south of Osoyoos, and the growing conditions here are unlike anywhere else in the country. Long, hot days. Cool nights. Soils ranging from sandy loam on the Black Sage Bench to clay and gravel on the Golden Mile. This is where BC's most serious reds come from — Syrah, Cabernet Franc, Merlot-based blends that can age a decade or more. If you want to understand what BC red wine is capable of, start here.

Naramata / Okanagan Falls

Naramata Bench runs north from Penticton along the east shore of Okanagan Lake, producing wines with more tension and freshness than the south — elegant reds, whites with real structure, and some of the better rosé in the province. Okanagan Falls, just south, sits at the head of Skaha Lake and has quietly become one of the most interesting clusters of small producers in BC. Dirty Laundry sits north in Summerland, where the lake's moderating effect is strongest.

Kelowna

The commercial heart of BC wine country, but some of the oldest vineyards in the province are here. Okanagan Lake moderates temperatures in both directions, giving Kelowna the range to produce everything from sparkling to late-harvest. The East Kelowna bench has a concentration of serious estate producers. West Kelowna, across the bridge, is where Mission Hill and Quails' Gate put BC wine on the international map. Organic farming is increasingly common. Tantalus and Sperling are the ones we keep coming back to.

Similkameen Valley

BC's smallest wine region and one of its most interesting. The valley runs east-west through the Cascade Mountains west of the Okanagan, with extreme diurnal temperature swings and gravelly alluvial soils that stress the vines in the best possible way. Organic farming is widespread here. Clos du Soleil has done as much as anyone to establish what this region can do. Orofino and Whispering Horse are two more reasons to pay attention to Cawston.

Gulf Islands

Cool, maritime, and producing small quantities from a cluster of boutique estate producers. The growing conditions are the opposite of the Okanagan — less heat, more rainfall, shorter seasons. The wines lean toward aromatic whites, skin-contact styles, and natural winemaking. A Sunday in August, out of Salt Spring Island, is exactly the kind of producer this region attracts: small farming operation, natural methods, genuinely distinctive wine unlike anything from the mainland.

Vancouver Island

The Cowichan Valley, just north of Victoria, is the most established sub-region. The climate is maritime and the seasons are shorter than the Okanagan, which means white wines and Pinot Noir are the natural strengths. Averill Creek makes some of the most serious wine coming off the island. Blue Grouse, also in the Cowichan Valley, has been at it for decades. The island is also home to some of BC's best distilleries — Shelter Point, Ampersand, and Macaloney's are all within a short drive of each other.
